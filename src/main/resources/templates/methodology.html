Our Calculation Methodology

We follow a systematic 5-step process to calculate your real buying power.

Step 1: Federal Tax Calculation

We apply the IRS progressive tax brackets for your filing status (single or married). For example, a single filer earning $100,000 in 2025 pays:

  • 10% on first $11,600 = $1,160
  • 12% on $11,601 - $47,150 = $4,266
  • 22% on $47,151 - $100,000 = $11,627
  • Total federal tax: $17,053 (17.05% effective rate)

We then subtract the standard deduction ($14,600 for single filers in 2025) before applying brackets.

Step 2: State and Local Taxes

Each state has different income tax structures:

  • No income tax: Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, etc.
  • Flat rate: Illinois (4.95%), Pennsylvania (3.07%), etc.
  • Progressive: California (1-13.3%), New York (4-10.9%), etc.

Some cities also impose local income taxes (e.g., New York City adds 3.078-3.876%). We calculate these based on official tax tables from each jurisdiction.

Step 3: Housing Costs

We estimate monthly housing costs using Numbeo's median rent data for each city. This represents the typical cost for a one-bedroom apartment in a standard neighborhood. For example:

  • San Francisco: ~$3,200/month
  • Austin: ~$2,100/month
  • Indianapolis: ~$1,150/month

Important: Actual rent varies significantly by neighborhood, apartment size, and amenities. Our estimates represent city-wide averages.

Step 4: Living Expenses

We calculate monthly living costs (groceries, utilities, transportation, healthcare) using Numbeo's cost of living indices. These are scaled relative to the national average. For example, if a city's cost of living index is 120, living expenses are approximately 20% higher than the national average.

We estimate base living expenses at approximately $1,200/month for a single person, then adjust by the city's cost index.

Step 5: Net Residual (Savings Potential)

Finally, we calculate your monthly residual:

Residual = Gross Salary - Federal Tax - State Tax - Local Tax - Housing - Living Costs

This residual represents your true savings potential or discretionary spending capacity. We then project this over 10 years with 7% compound growth to estimate long-term wealth accumulation.

What We Don't Include

To maintain simplicity and broad applicability, we intentionally exclude certain factors.

We use only the standard deduction, not itemized deductions (mortgage interest, charitable contributions, etc.). Most taxpayers take the standard deduction, making this the most representative approach.

Employer-Specific Benefits

401(k) matching, HSA contributions, stock options, and bonuses vary dramatically by employer. Users can add these via our Simulation Lab, but we don't include them in default calculations.
